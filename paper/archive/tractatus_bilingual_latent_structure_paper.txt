Bilingual Structure Latents in the Tractatus: Aligning German and English
Propositions with Shared Logical Supervision

Author: Mark Alfred Griffiths
Project: Tractatus Latent Logic


ABSTRACT

This paper describes a bilingual extension of the split-latent variational
autoencoder experiment on Wittgenstein's Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus. The
original experiment asked whether the hierarchical numbering system of the
Tractatus could be used as weak supervision for learning a compact latent
representation of formal structure. The bilingual experiment asks a narrower
follow-up question: when German and English proposition texts are reconstructed
as separate language-conditioned realizations, but share the same hierarchy
supervision, does the structure latent align the two languages by proposition
identity?

The dataset contains 526 numbered propositions. Each proposition has an English
text and a German text, giving 1052 flattened language-specific training and
evaluation samples. The model uses a split latent space: a 24-dimensional text
latent for reconstruction and an 8-dimensional structure latent for hierarchy
prediction. In bilingual runs, the decoder is conditioned on language, while the
structural targets are shared across German and English by proposition id.

The strongest supported result is that the bilingual model learns
proposition-level structure latents that align German and English realizations
under shared Tractatus hierarchy supervision. Even with no explicit bilingual
alignment loss, cross-language retrieval is already strong: top-1 proposition
retrieval is 0.9211 and mean reciprocal rank is 0.9575. With moderate alignment
regularization at lambda_language_alignment = 0.10, retrieval improves to top-1
0.9363 and MRR 0.9621. The best hierarchy scores occur at
lambda_language_alignment = 0.03. High alignment at 1.00 degrades both hierarchy
prediction and retrieval.

The result does not show that the model understands Wittgenstein, and it does
not establish general language-invariant logic. It shows something more limited
and more concrete: under aligned German and English proposition texts, and under
shared Tractatus hierarchy supervision, the learned structure latent tracks
formal proposition position across languages.


1. INTRODUCTION

The Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus is a useful test case because its formal
layout is unusually explicit. The propositions are not simply placed one after
another. They are numbered hierarchically. Proposition 4.12 is related to
4.121, 4.1211, and 4.1212. Proposition 5 is related to propositions beginning
with 5.1, 5.2, and so on. The numbering system gives a formal scaffold: parent
relations, depth, siblings, descendants, and textual successor relations.

The original split-latent experiment used the English text of each proposition
and the hierarchy implied by the numbering system. It showed that a model could
learn an 8-dimensional structure latent that carried useful information about
formal position. But that left an important question unresolved. Was the
structure latent genuinely tracking formal position, or was it merely exploiting
English lexical regularities that happened to correlate with proposition number
and section?

The bilingual experiment addresses that question by adding the German text of
the same propositions. Each proposition now has two textual realizations. The
English and German strings differ lexically and syntactically, but they share a
proposition id and therefore share structural targets. The model is required to
reconstruct the text in the appropriate language, while the structure latent is
trained to predict the same hierarchy-derived targets for both realizations.

This creates a simple operational test. If the structure latent is only an
English lexical embedding, then German and English versions of the same
proposition should not necessarily appear near one another in structure space.
If, however, the structure latent is carrying proposition-level formal position,
then the German and English realizations of the same proposition should align.

The central claim of this paper is therefore deliberately modest:

    The bilingual model learns proposition-level structure latents that align
    German and English realizations under shared Tractatus hierarchy
    supervision.

This should not be inflated into a claim about general language-invariant logic.
The dataset is aligned by proposition id. The supervision comes from the
Tractatus numbering system. The evaluation is in-sample on this structured
corpus. The result is evidence for formal-position tracking in this setting, not
for general philosophical understanding.


2. RELATION TO THE MONOLINGUAL SPLIT-LATENT RESULT

The monolingual paper introduced the main modelling idea. A single latent vector
is a poor interpretive object when it is asked to do everything at once. It must
support text reconstruction, but it is also asked to predict hierarchy. These
objectives can interfere. A latent vector useful for reconstructing words need
not be the same vector that best represents formal position in the Tractatus
numbering tree.

The retained architecture therefore splits the latent vector into two parts:

    z_text:       24 dimensions
    z_structure:  8 dimensions

The text latent is used by the decoder. The structure latent is used by
hierarchy-prediction heads. In the retained split model, the decoder does not
receive z_structure. This prevents the structure subspace from becoming an
unrestricted reconstruction channel.

The bilingual experiment keeps this separation but changes the data and the
reconstruction problem. Instead of one English row per proposition, the dataset
contains one row per proposition with two text fields:

    texts.en: English proposition text
    texts.de: German proposition text

The training loader flattens these rows into language-specific samples. For
example, proposition 4.121 produces one English sample and one German sample.
Both samples have different text, but both share the same proposition id,
parent_id, depth, next_id, children, siblings, ancestor_chain, and child_count.

The bilingual experiment is therefore a direct companion to the monolingual
paper. It tests whether the structure subspace remains meaningful when the
model sees two language realizations of the same formal proposition.


3. BILINGUAL DATASET

The bilingual dataset is stored at:

    tractatus_latent_logic/data/tractatus_bilingual.json

It is built from the Project Gutenberg Tractatus source. The parser extracts
English propositions from \PropositionE entries and German propositions from
\PropositionG entries. The resulting dataset has one structural row per
numbered proposition. Each row contains proposition-level metadata and a texts
object containing the two language realizations.

A simplified row has this form:

    id: "4.121"
    parent_id: "4.12"
    depth: 4
    texts.en: "Propositions cannot represent the logical form..."
    texts.de: "Die Saetze koennen die logische Form nicht darstellen..."
    next_id: "4.1211"
    children: ["4.1211", "4.1212"]
    siblings: ["4.122", "4.123"]
    ancestor_chain: ["4", "4.1", "4.12"]
    child_count: 2

Dataset statistics:

    proposition rows:          526
    flattened text samples:    1052
    languages:                 de, en
    first proposition:          1
    last proposition:           7

Depth distribution:

    depth 1:   7
    depth 2:   25
    depth 3:   124
    depth 4:   245
    depth 5:   118
    depth 6:   7

Top-level section distribution:

    section 1:   7
    section 2:   79
    section 3:   74
    section 4:   109
    section 5:   151
    section 6:   105
    section 7:   1

The schema matters. The model does not receive an unstructured bilingual text
corpus. It receives aligned proposition rows. The German and English texts are
different surface realizations, but the proposition id and the hierarchy targets
are shared. This makes the experiment a test of proposition-level structural
alignment, not a test of unsupervised translation.


4. MODEL AND TRAINING OBJECTIVE

The bilingual model is a split-latent recurrent VAE. The encoder reads the text
of one proposition in one language. It outputs a latent distribution, from which
the model samples a latent vector. That latent vector is split into z_text and
z_structure.

The high-level flow is:

    proposition text
      -> encoder
      -> z_text and z_structure
      -> language-conditioned decoder from z_text
      -> reconstructed proposition text

The structure latent is passed to auxiliary hierarchy heads:

    parent_head(z_structure)
    depth_head(z_structure)
    next_head(z_structure)
    child_count_head(z_structure)

The bilingual addition is language conditioning. The decoder receives a learned
language embedding, so it knows whether it is reconstructing German or English.
This is important because German and English reconstruction require different
lexical and syntactic information. The model should not be forced to collapse
all language-specific information into a single shared text representation.

The retained bilingual architecture uses:

    z_text:       24 dimensions
    z_structure:  8 dimensions
    total latent: 32 dimensions

The loss combines reconstruction, KL regularization, hierarchy prediction, and
an optional same-proposition cross-language alignment term:

    L = reconstruction_loss
      + beta_text * KL(z_text)
      + beta_structure * KL(z_structure)
      + lambda_parent * parent_loss
      + lambda_depth * depth_loss
      + lambda_next * next_loss
      + lambda_child * child_count_loss
      + lambda_language_alignment * alignment_loss

The alignment term is applied to z_structure for same-id German and English
samples:

    L_align = mse(z_structure_en(id), z_structure_de(id))

The sweep varies lambda_language_alignment while holding the rest of the
training setup fixed:

    z_text:          24
    z_structure:      8
    epochs:          80
    batch_size:      32
    beta_text:     0.01
    beta_structure: 0.05
    lambda_parent: 0.2
    lambda_depth:  0.1
    lambda_next:   0.2
    lambda_child:  0.02

The tested alignment values are:

    lambda_language_alignment = 0.00, 0.03, 0.10, 0.30, 1.00


5. EVALUATION METRICS

The evaluation uses both hierarchy metrics and bilingual alignment metrics.

Hierarchy metrics measure whether z_structure supports prediction of the formal
relations derived from the numbering system:

    parent accuracy: predicted parent proposition
    depth accuracy:  predicted hierarchy depth
    next accuracy:   predicted next proposition in textual order

Bilingual retrieval metrics measure whether German and English realizations of
the same proposition are close in z_structure. For each German structure latent,
the evaluator retrieves the nearest English structure latent and checks whether
it has the same proposition id. The same is done in the English-to-German
direction. The combined metrics are:

    cross_language_top1_id_accuracy
    cross_language_mrr
    mean_same_id_cross_language_distance

The top-1 metric is the clearest operational test. It asks whether the nearest
opposite-language structure latent is the matching proposition. MRR is less
strict but captures whether the matching proposition is ranked near the top even
when it is not first.

The evaluation also reports reconstruction loss and perplexity. These are not
the main target of the paper, but they are useful for detecting whether an
alignment setting damages the model's ability to reconstruct text.

Several points matter.

First, the no-alignment baseline is already strong. With
lambda_language_alignment = 0.00, cross-language top-1 proposition retrieval is
0.9211 and MRR is 0.9575. This means that shared hierarchy supervision alone is
sufficient to induce substantial bilingual alignment in z_structure.

Second, explicit alignment helps only in moderation. The best bilingual
retrieval is obtained at lambda_language_alignment = 0.10:

    cross_language_top1_id_accuracy: 0.9363
    cross_language_mrr:              0.9621

Third, the best hierarchy scores occur at lambda_language_alignment = 0.03:

    parent_accuracy: 0.8774
    depth_accuracy:  0.9677
    next_accuracy:   0.6103

Fourth, high alignment damages the model. At lambda_language_alignment = 1.00,
performance falls to:

    parent_accuracy: 0.5494
    depth_accuracy:  0.7196
    next_accuracy:   0.3707
    top1 retrieval:  0.7890
    MRR:             0.8561

The result is therefore not that more alignment is always better. The better
interpretation is that z_structure needs enough cross-language pressure to bring
same-id propositions together, but not so much pressure that it loses the
fine-grained distinctions needed for hierarchy prediction.

Directional retrieval gives the same picture:

    lambda  de->en top1  en->de top1  de->en MRR  en->de MRR
    0.00    0.9259       0.9163       0.9604      0.9547
    0.03    0.9202       0.9068       0.9561      0.9501
    0.10    0.9411       0.9316       0.9640      0.9603
    0.30    0.8460       0.8650       0.8953      0.9085
    1.00    0.7871       0.7909       0.8569      0.8553

The effect is balanced. At the best retrieval setting, German-to-English top-1
is 0.9411 and English-to-German top-1 is 0.9316. The model is not merely mapping
one language into the other in a one-sided way.

By-language structure metrics are also similar across German and English:

This supports the view that the structure latent is not only an English-side
artifact. Both languages support broadly similar hierarchy prediction.

The language-coloured plot is useful because it checks a failure mode. If the
structure latent were dominated by language identity, one would expect German
and English samples to separate strongly. Instead, the retrieval metrics show
that same-id German and English latents are usually near one another in the
structure space.

The depth-coloured plot is useful for a different reason. It checks whether the
same latent space still carries formal hierarchy information. The sweep results
show that alignment can improve retrieval while also weakening some hierarchy
metrics. Visualization should therefore be read alongside the quantitative
scores, not in place of them.


8. PROPOSITION TRACKING AND FORMAL LOGICAL POSITION

The most important interpretive question is what the bilingual alignment means.
It is tempting to say that the model has learned language-invariant logic. That
would be too strong.

The safer interpretation is proposition tracking under shared formal
supervision. The Tractatus numbering system encodes a formal organization of the
book. Proposition ids are not arbitrary labels in the ordinary sense; they mark
positions in a hierarchy. If German and English versions of proposition 4.121
are close in z_structure, and if z_structure also supports parent, depth, and
next prediction, then the latent is tracking something about the proposition's
formal role in the Tractatus.

This is not the same as philosophical understanding. The model is not shown to
understand what proposition 4.121 means. It is not shown to reason from that
proposition to another. It is not shown to generalize to unseen philosophical
texts. It is shown to place two language realizations of the same numbered
proposition near one another in a learned structure space trained on the shared
hierarchy.

That result still matters. It answers a concrete objection to the monolingual
paper. If the English-only structure latent were merely a lexical embedding,
then introducing German should disrupt proposition-level alignment. Instead,
the bilingual results show strong alignment. The no-alignment baseline already
retrieves matching cross-language propositions with top-1 0.9211. Moderate
alignment improves this to 0.9363.

The best description is therefore:

    z_structure tracks proposition-level formal position across German and
    English realizations under shared Tractatus hierarchy supervision.

This is a meaningful result about the geometry of the learned representation.
It is not a claim that the model appreciates logic in the human philosophical
sense.


9. DISCUSSION

The bilingual experiment strengthens the original split-latent result in three
ways.

First, it shows that the structure latent is not tied only to English text. The
model reconstructs German and English through language-conditioned decoding,
while the structural heads operate on a shared z_structure. The resulting
cross-language retrieval scores are high.

Second, it shows that explicit alignment is not the whole story. The
lambda_language_alignment = 0.00 baseline already performs strongly. This
suggests that shared hierarchy supervision itself pulls same-proposition German
and English structure latents into compatible regions. The alignment loss then
acts as an additional pressure, not as the only source of bilingual structure.

Third, it reveals a tradeoff. A small amount of alignment can help. Too much
alignment harms the representation. At lambda_language_alignment = 1.00, the
model loses important structural distinctions. This is consistent with the
purpose of z_structure: it must align same-id language realizations, but it must
also keep different propositions apart in ways that preserve hierarchy.

The result also clarifies the role of reconstruction. The best reconstruction
loss occurs at lambda_language_alignment = 0.30, with reconstruction loss 1.1883
and perplexity 3.2815. But that setting is not the best structure model. Its
cross-language top-1 retrieval falls to 0.8555, and its next accuracy falls to
0.5428. This confirms a central lesson of the monolingual paper: reconstruction
quality alone is not enough to judge whether the latent space carries the
intended formal structure.


10. LIMITATIONS

The limitations are substantial.

The dataset is small. It contains 526 propositions and 1052 flattened bilingual
samples. This is enough for a controlled proof-of-principle experiment, but not
enough to support broad claims about multilingual reasoning.

The German and English texts are aligned by proposition id. This is a favorable
setting for cross-language retrieval. The experiment does not show that the
model could discover such alignments from unpaired text.

The structural supervision is derived from the Tractatus numbering system. The
model is not discovering the hierarchy from raw text alone. It is learning a
latent representation under explicit weak supervision from parent, depth, next,
and child-count targets.

The evaluation is in-sample. The reported retrieval and hierarchy metrics do not
establish out-of-sample generalization to unseen propositions, unseen sections,
or other philosophical works.

The result is specific to the Tractatus. The book is unusually structured, and
its German and English versions are parallel realizations of the same numbered
work. Other texts may not provide comparable formal scaffolding.

The model uses a simple word-level modelling setup rather than a modern
multilingual tokenizer or pretrained multilingual encoder. This is useful for
interpretability, but it limits direct comparison with contemporary multilingual
language models.

Finally, the philosophical interpretation must remain modest. The model does
not understand Wittgenstein. It does not prove language-invariant logic. It
learns a compact structure latent that aligns bilingual proposition realizations
under shared hierarchy supervision.


11. CONCLUSION

The bilingual experiment provides a focused extension of the original
split-latent Tractatus model. The original result showed that an 8-dimensional
structure latent could recover useful information about the formal hierarchy of
the English Tractatus. The bilingual result shows that this structure latent can
also align German and English realizations of the same propositions.

The strongest evidence is the cross-language retrieval result. With no explicit
alignment loss, top-1 retrieval is already 0.9211 and MRR is 0.9575. With
moderate alignment at lambda_language_alignment = 0.10, retrieval improves to
top-1 0.9363 and MRR 0.9621. The best hierarchy metrics occur at
lambda_language_alignment = 0.03. High alignment at 1.00 degrades both hierarchy
prediction and retrieval.

The conclusion is therefore not that the model has learned general logic. The
conclusion is that, in this aligned bilingual Tractatus setting, the split-latent
model learns a proposition-level structural representation shared across German
and English realizations.


12. FUTURE WORK

The immediate next step is to repeat the best settings over multiple random
seeds. The present sweep establishes the shape of the tradeoff, but it does not
measure variance.

A second step is to add held-out evaluations. Section-level or proposition-level
splits would test whether the learned structure representation generalizes
beyond the exact propositions used in training.

A third step is to compare against multilingual sentence embeddings. Such a
comparison would help distinguish what is gained by the explicit split-latent
architecture and hierarchy supervision.

A fourth step is to test weaker alignment conditions. For example, the model
could be trained with partial bilingual coverage, missing translations, or
unpaired propositions.

A fifth step is to extend the dataset to additional translations if reliable
public-domain sources are available. This would test whether the structural
latent remains stable beyond German and English.

Finally, similar methods could be applied to other formally organized texts.
The key requirement is not that the text be philosophical, but that it have a
meaningful explicit structure that can be used as weak supervision.
