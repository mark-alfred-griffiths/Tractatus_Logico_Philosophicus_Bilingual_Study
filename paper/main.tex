\begin{abstract}
Wittgenstein's Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus (1922) is suited to structural modelling because its 526 propositions encode a tree-like scaffold of hierarchy depth, local families, sequential order and child structure. This paper uses that scaffold as weak supervision for learning compact latent representations of proposition position, and asks whether such representations align German and English realisations of the same proposition. The monolingual study uses English texts; the bilingual study uses German and English versions, yielding 1052 language-specific samples aligned by proposition ID.

The retained model is a split-latent variational autoencoder (VAE) with gated recurrent units (GRU) encoder and decoder. Its latent space separates a 24-dimensional text latent, used for reconstruction, from an 8-dimensional structure latent, used by structural heads predicting parent proposition, depth, next proposition and child count. In the bilingual model, the decoder is language-conditioned and an optional alignment loss pulls same-ID German and English structure latents together.

The monolingual seed sweep (10 seeds, regularisation at 0.05) shows that the split structure latent recovers hierarchy-relevant information. Parent accuracy is 0.6983 $\pm$ 0.0152, depth accuracy 0.9274 $\pm$ 0.0275 and next-proposition accuracy 0.3724 $\pm$ 0.0198, with reconstruction loss 1.2923 $\pm$ 0.0194 and perplexity 3.6416 $\pm$ 0.0706. Sibling propositions are closer in structure space (2.0656 $\pm$ 0.0342) than unrelated propositions (5.5574 $\pm$ 0.0532).

The bilingual sweep across ten seeds and five alignment strengths shows strong cross-language proposition tracking. With no alignment loss, Top-1 retrieval is 0.9348 $\pm$ 0.0115 and MRR is 0.9650 $\pm$ 0.0066. Light alignment ($\lambda$ = 0.03) gives the strongest mean scores: Top-1 retrieval 0.9365 $\pm$ 0.0103, MRR 0.9662 $\pm$ 0.0058, parent accuracy 0.8608, depth accuracy 0.9643 and next accuracy 0.6189. High alignment ($\lambda$ = 1.00) degrades retrieval (0.7943 $\pm$ 0.0188) and MRR (0.8577 $\pm$ 0.0130). Shared hierarchy supervision therefore induces a compact proposition-level structure latent that aligns bilingual realisations without demonstrating general language-invariant logic or philosophical understanding of Wittgenstein's text.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Wittgenstein's Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus is unusually suited to computational structural analysis because its propositions are not arranged merely as a linear sequence of philosophical remarks. They are organised by an explicit decimal numbering system that encodes relations of hierarchy and dependence.\textsuperscript{1,2} However, because the relation between tree-structured and sequential readings remains interpretively contested, the present paper treats the numbering system as a formal scaffold rather than as a complete philosophical interpretation.\textsuperscript{3} A proposition such as 4.121 is therefore not only a sentence in the text, but also a formal position within the work's architecture. The numbering system indicates depth, parent propositions, local proposition families, sequential order, and child structure. This makes the Tractatus a rare philosophical corpus in which formal textual organisation can be operationalised directly.

Philosophical texts are usually studied in terms of argument, meaning, interpretation, and historical context. Yet some texts also possess explicit formal architectures that shape how their arguments unfold. Hierarchical and ordered documents occur not only in philosophy, but also in legal, theological, mathematical, and technical writing. Such texts raise a computational question: can a neural representation learn not only the wording of textual units, but also their position within an explicit document structure? The Tractatus provides a constrained and well-defined test case for this question because its proposition numbers supply a formal scaffold against which learned representations can be evaluated. Earlier computational work has assembled multilingual versions of the Tractatus as a parallel corpus and visualised proposition-level relations; the present study instead uses the numbering scaffold as weak supervision for latent representation learning.\textsuperscript{4}

This paper asks whether the numbering scaffold of the Tractatus can be used as weak supervision for learning a compact latent representation of proposition position. The aim is not to show that a model understands Wittgenstein's philosophy, nor that it discovers logical form from raw text alone. The claim is narrower: hierarchy-derived targets from the proposition numbering system can be used to train and evaluate a latent representation of formal textual position. In this sense, the task is supervised representation learning over a formally structured philosophical text, rather than unsupervised philosophical interpretation.

To test this, the study uses a split-latent variational autoencoder with recurrent encoder and decoder components, drawing on variational autoencoding and recurrent encoder-decoder modelling.\textsuperscript{5,6} The latent space is divided into a 24-dimensional text latent and an 8-dimensional structure latent. The text latent is used for reconstructing proposition wording, while the structure latent is trained to predict hierarchy-derived targets, including parent proposition, hierarchy depth, next proposition, and child count. This design encourages a division of labour between surface reconstruction and formal position. The purpose of the split is to make the hierarchy-carrying component of the model measurable and interpretable, rather than allowing all information to be absorbed into a single undifferentiated latent representation.

The first experiment is monolingual and uses English proposition texts. It tests whether the compact structure latent can recover hierarchy-relevant information from the English version of the Tractatus. The relevant question is not whether every formal relation is predicted with equal accuracy, but whether the learned structure latent captures broad hierarchy, local parenthood, and proposition neighbourhoods. This experiment establishes whether the model learns a useful representation of formal position in a single-language setting, and whether the structure latent behaves differently from a reconstruction-oriented text latent.

The second experiment extends the same framework to a bilingual setting. German and English versions of each proposition are aligned by proposition ID, producing two textual realisations of the same formal position. The bilingual model uses shared hierarchy-derived targets, a shared encoder, a shared bilingual vocabulary, and a language-conditioned decoder. It also includes an optional alignment loss that can pull German and English structure latents for the same proposition closer together. This setup makes it possible to test whether shared hierarchy supervision can induce proposition-level alignment across languages without requiring open-ended translation modelling or pretrained multilingual embeddings.

The bilingual question is important because it separates surface language from formal proposition position. If German and English versions of the same numbered proposition occupy nearby regions of the structure latent space, then the model is not merely encoding language-specific wording. Rather, it is learning a representation that tracks formal position across different linguistic realisations. At the same time, cross-language retrieval of proposition IDs should be interpreted carefully. It is not evidence of general multilingual reasoning or philosophical understanding. It is evidence that, under aligned proposition IDs and shared hierarchy supervision, a compact structure latent can support bilingual proposition alignment.

The results show that the monolingual structure latent captures hierarchy-relevant information, with depth recovered most strongly, parent prediction meaningful, and next-proposition prediction weaker. The bilingual results show strong German-English proposition retrieval in the structure latent. Importantly, explicit language alignment is not monotonically beneficial. No or light alignment gives the strongest overall performance, while high alignment pressure degrades both hierarchy prediction and cross-language retrieval. This suggests that the structure latent must balance cross-language invariance with fine-grained proposition discrimination. A useful latent space must bring German and English versions of the same proposition together without collapsing distinctions between neighbouring propositions.

The contribution of this paper is therefore methodological. It shows that the Tractatus numbering system can be operationalised as weak supervision for interpretable neural representation learning. It demonstrates that a split-latent VAE can partially separate reconstruction-oriented textual information from hierarchy-oriented structural information. It also shows that bilingual versions of the same proposition can align in a shared structure latent when they are tied by common hierarchy-derived targets. The broader implication is that formally organised (e.g. philosophical, mathematical and legal) texts can serve as constrained test cases for studying how textual hierarchy, latent representation, and bilingual alignment interact.

The remainder of the paper describes the corpus construction, tokenisation, split-latent architecture, bilingual language conditioning, alignment objective, training procedure, and evaluation metrics. It then reports monolingual hierarchy recovery, bilingual proposition retrieval, directional retrieval balance, by-language structure prediction, reconstruction diagnostics, latent-distance analyses, and PCA visualisations. The final sections interpret the findings as evidence for a compact proposition-level structure latent which captures formal textual position.

\section{Method}
\subsection{Experimental Design}
This study uses Wittgenstein's Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus as a controlled structured-text corpus to test whether the work's explicit proposition numbering system can provide weak supervision for learning latent representations of formal position. The experiments comprise two parts of the same representation-learning study. The monolingual experiment tests whether a compact structure latent can recover hierarchy-derived targets from English proposition text. The bilingual experiment tests whether German and English versions of the same numbered proposition align in the learned structure latent while preserving the same formal hierarchy. The task is therefore framed as supervised representation learning over an explicitly structured philosophical text, not as unsupervised discovery of philosophical meaning. The narrower methodological claim is that a compact hierarchy-supervised structure latent can learn proposition-level formal position and can align English and German realisations of the same numbered proposition.

\subsection{Corpus Construction and Hierarchy-Derived Targets}
The corpus was constructed from the Project Gutenberg electronic text of the Tractatus, which provides the machine-readable source text used for proposition extraction.\textsuperscript{7} Each proposition is associated with a proposition ID, such as 4.121, which encodes its formal position in the book's hierarchy. The retained dataset contains all 526 propositions in the English version and, correspondingly, 1052 German and English propositions in the bilingual version. In the monolingual experiment, each row contains a proposition ID, English proposition text, and hierarchy-derived targets. In the bilingual experiment, each row contains the proposition ID, German text, English text, and shared hierarchy-derived targets. German and English versions are aligned by proposition ID, so the bilingual setup evaluates proposition-level structure alignment rather than unsupervised translation.

Hierarchy-derived targets are obtained from the proposition numbering system. The parent proposition is the immediately superordinate proposition in the numbering hierarchy. Depth records the hierarchical level of the proposition. The next-proposition target identifies the following proposition in reading order, and child count records the number of direct subordinate propositions. German and English versions of the same proposition share the same proposition ID, parent, depth, next-proposition target, child count, children, siblings, and ancestor chain.

A simplified bilingual row has this form:

\begin{verbatim}
id: "4.121"
parent_id: "4.12"
depth: 4
texts.en: "Propositions cannot represent the logical form..."
texts.de: "Die Saetze koennen die logische Form nicht darstellen..."
next_id: "4.1211"
children: ["4.1211", "4.1212"]
siblings: ["4.122", "4.123"]
ancestor_chain: ["4", "4.1", "4.12"]
child_count: 2
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{Tokenisation and Text Representation}
Proposition text is tokenised at word level after lower-casing and punctuation handling. A vocabulary is constructed from the retained corpus and tokens are mapped to integer IDs. A padding token is used to support mini-batch training with variable-length proposition texts, and an unknown-token marker is used for tokens not present in the vocabulary. Token IDs are passed through a learned embedding layer. In the bilingual model, German and English tokens share the same token embedding table. This token embedding layer is not a pretrained multilingual embedding; it learns corpus-specific representations for both German and English vocabulary items during training.

\subsection{Split-Latent VAE Architecture}
The model is a recurrent variational autoencoder with a split latent representation. The encoder and decoder are implemented with gated recurrent units, following recurrent encoder-decoder sequence modelling.\textsuperscript{6} The encoder reads one proposition text at a time and maps the input sequence into latent parameters. The sampled latent representation is divided into a 24-dimensional text latent and an 8-dimensional structure latent. The text latent is used for text reconstruction, whereas the structure latent is used for hierarchy prediction. This split-latent design separates reconstruction of proposition text from prediction of formal proposition position, making the structure latent more interpretable.

The recurrent encoder receives tokenised proposition text after tokens have been embedded through the learned token embedding layer. The final recurrent state is projected into separate means and log-variances for the text and structure latents. Latent samples are drawn using the VAE reparameterisation trick, allowing signals to backpropagate through the sampling step.\textsuperscript{5} The encoder is shared across samples; in the bilingual model it processes both German and English proposition texts.

The decoder reconstructs the input proposition text from the text latent and does not receive the structure latent. This design discourages the structure latent from being used as a general reconstruction feature and reserves it for the structural prediction heads. In the monolingual model the decoder reconstructs only English text. In the bilingual model the decoder is language-conditioned: a per-language embedding indicates whether the decoder should reconstruct German or English. The text latent plus an English language embedding is used to reconstruct English, whereas the text latent plus a German language embedding is used to reconstruct German. At each time step, the recurrent decoder outputs logits over the shared vocabulary. Applying a softmax yields token probabilities, and predicted token IDs are mapped back to strings using the corpus vocabulary.

The structure latent is passed to task-specific prediction heads. These hierarchy heads predict parent proposition, depth, next proposition, and child count, and operate on the structure latent rather than on the text latent. Parent prediction, next-proposition prediction, and depth prediction are treated as categorical prediction tasks. Child-count prediction is treated as a supervised structural target. The structural heads force the 8-dimensional latent to carry information about formal proposition position. This use of related supervised heads over a shared latent representation is consistent with multitask learning, in which auxiliary targets shape a common representation.\textsuperscript{8}

\subsection{Bilingual Conditioning and Structure Alignment}
The bilingual model extends the monolingual split-latent architecture by adding German and English input texts, a shared bilingual token vocabulary, a shared encoder for both languages, a language-conditioned decoder, shared hierarchy targets by proposition ID, and, where enabled, a cross-language structure alignment loss. German and English versions of the same proposition are treated as different textual realisations of the same formal proposition position. The structure latent is encouraged to represent language-shared formal position while the decoder handles language-specific reconstruction. The hierarchy targets used by the structural prediction heads are identical for German and English samples with the same proposition ID.

Each bilingual sample carries a language label, English or German, which is mapped to a learned language embedding. This language embedding is concatenated with, or otherwise supplied alongside, the text latent during decoding. The purpose of language conditioning is to tell the decoder which surface language to reconstruct, reducing pressure on the 8-dimensional structure latent to account for language identity. The intended division of labour is that token embeddings and the text latent support language-specific reconstruction, the language embedding specifies the reconstruction language, and the structure latent supports hierarchy prediction.

The bilingual model can also apply a cross-language structure alignment loss to German and English structure latents for the same proposition ID. This loss penalises distance between same-ID English and German structure vectors and is calculated as a mean squared error between the German and English structure latents for the same proposition. The purpose of the term is to test whether explicit cross-language pressure improves proposition-level alignment. Alignment strength is controlled by the language-alignment weight $\lambda$. The sweep tests alignment strengths of 0.00, 0.03, 0.10, 0.30, and 1.00. The no-alignment condition tests whether shared hierarchy supervision alone can induce bilingual structure alignment.

\subsection{Training Objectives and Experimental Settings}
The monolingual experiment uses English proposition texts. The model receives English text and hierarchy-derived targets. The retained monolingual configuration uses text latent = 24, structure latent = 8, $\beta_{\mathrm{text}}$ = 0.01, and $\beta_{\mathrm{structure}}$ = 0.05. The monolingual experiment evaluates whether the 8-dimensional structure latent captures hierarchy-relevant information. Reported monolingual metrics include parent accuracy, depth accuracy, next-proposition accuracy, reconstruction loss, perplexity, KL structure, sibling distance, parent-child distance, and unrelated-proposition distance.

The full bilingual objective includes reconstruction loss, a KL penalty for the text latent, a KL penalty for the structure latent, parent prediction loss, depth prediction loss, next-proposition prediction loss, child-count loss, and, where enabled, the cross-language structure alignment loss. The text and structure KL terms are weighted separately. The retained bilingual sweep uses $\beta_{\mathrm{text}}$ = 0.001, $\beta_{\mathrm{structure}}$=0.05, $\lambda_{\mathrm{parent}}$=0.2, $\lambda_{\mathrm{depth}}$=0.1, $\lambda_{\mathrm{next}}$=0.2, and $\lambda_{\mathrm{child}}$=0.02. The language-alignment weight is varied while the other settings are held fixed. The monolingual loss is the bilingual loss without the bilingual structure alignment loss.

\subsection{Evaluation and Statistical Reporting}
Reconstruction quality is measured using reconstruction loss and perplexity. Structural prediction is evaluated using parent accuracy, depth accuracy, next-proposition accuracy, and child-count performance where applicable. Cross-language alignment is evaluated using proposition retrieval. In German-to-English retrieval, a German structure latent is used as the query and English structure latents are ranked by distance. Retrieval is correct if the nearest English latent has the same proposition ID; English-to-German retrieval is the reverse direction. Top-1 retrieval measures whether the nearest cross-language neighbour is the same-ID proposition. Mean reciprocal rank (MRR) measures how highly the correct same-ID proposition appears in the ranked retrieval list. Directional retrieval is reported separately to check whether alignment is balanced across English and German. By-language parent accuracy is also reported to check whether one language dominates structural prediction.

The learned structure latent is analysed geometrically for diagnostic purposes. PCA projections are used as representative visual diagnostics and are not treated as aggregate evidence. Each PCA figure corresponds to a specific trained seed and alignment condition. Language-coloured PCA plots test whether English and German samples separate into isolated language clusters. Depth-coloured PCA plots test whether the structure latent reflects hierarchy depth. Quantitative retrieval and hierarchy metrics are treated as the main evidence, with PCA used only to support interpretation of the learned latent geometry.

Reported quantitative claims are based on seed sweeps rather than single runs. The monolingual retrieval model is evaluated across seeds 0-9. Similarly, the bilingual alignment sweep is evaluated across ten seeds 0-9 for each alignment weight. Results are reported as means with SDs where appropriate.

\section{Results}
The main result is that the learned 8-dimensional structure latent captures formal proposition position and supports English-German proposition alignment. This, of course, does not mean that model comprehends Wittgenstein's logic but is instead evidence that hierarchy-derived supervision can produce an interpretable proposition-level structure latent.

\subsection{Monolingual Split-Latent Results}
The monolingual experiment establishes that the split-latent architecture can recover hierarchy-relevant structure from English proposition text. It also motivates the separation of structure from text reconstruction. See Table 1. and Figure 1. for these results. Depth prediction is very strong, indicating that the model learns broad hierarchical level well. Parent prediction is moderate-to-strong, demonstrating that the model captures local hierarchical dependency. Next-proposition prediction is notably weaker, which is expected because order is more fine-grained and may not be recoverable from text alone. This supports the claim that structure is not a reconstruction feature, it contains hierarchy-relevant geometry. The distance metrics are also useful because they check latent geometry directly. Siblings are much closer than unrelated propositions in the retained monolingual structure space.

\subsection{Bilingual Alignment Results}
The bilingual experiment asks whether the structure latent can align English and German versions of the same numbered proposition while preserving the formal hierarchy of the Tractatus. The core seed-sweep results are reported in Table 2 and visualised in Figure 2. Explicit language alignment is not required for strong proposition retrieval. With $\lambda$ = 0.00, the model retrieves the correct cross-language proposition with Top-1 accuracy of 0.9348 $\pm$ 0.0115 and MRR of 0.9650 $\pm$ 0.0066. Thus, shared hierarchy supervision alone induces substantial English--German alignment in the structure latent. Light alignment gives the strongest mean retrieval: at $\lambda$ = 0.03, Top-1 accuracy rises to 0.9365 $\pm$ 0.0103 and MRR to 0.9662 $\pm$ 0.0058. However, increasing alignment pressure beyond this point steadily degrades performance. At high alignment, $\lambda$ = 1.00, Top-1 accuracy falls to 0.7943 $\pm$ 0.0188 and MRR to 0.8577 $\pm$ 0.0130. This shows that stronger explicit alignment does not monotonically improve bilingual retrieval.

The same pattern is visible in the hierarchy-prediction metrics. As shown in Table 2 and Figure 3, parent, depth, and next-proposition accuracies are strongest under no or light alignment and deteriorate as $\lambda$ increases. Depth is recovered most reliably, parent prediction remains strong in the low-alignment settings, and next-proposition prediction is the weakest structural task. The retrieval--structure trade-off is summarised directly in Figure 4: no or light alignment occupies the strongest region of the plot, while $\lambda$ = 0.30 and especially $\lambda$ = 1.00 reduce both cross-language retrieval and mean structure-prediction accuracy. This suggests that the structure latent must balance cross-language invariance against the preservation of fine-grained hierarchy distinctions.

Directional retrieval was balanced across languages. Table 3 shows that German-to-English and English-to-German retrieval produce closely matched Top-1 and MRR scores at each alignment setting. This rules out a one-sided interpretation in which the model simply projects German into an English-centred structure space, or English into a German-centred structure space. Instead, both retrieval directions are supported by the same shared structure latent. By-language structure prediction shows the same pattern. As reported in Table 4, German and English parent, depth, and next-proposition accuracies are closely matched across the strongest settings, indicating that the structure latent is not preferentially fitted to one language.

Reconstruction metrics provide useful diagnostics but are not sufficient for model selection. Table 5 and Figure 5 show that reconstruction loss and perplexity remain broadly stable under no or light alignment, but worsen at high alignment. Because the purpose of the model is not only to reconstruct proposition text but to isolate formal structure, reconstruction quality alone cannot identify the best model. The relevant model is the one that preserves both hierarchy prediction and cross-language proposition identity. The latent-distance diagnostics in Table 6 reinforce this interpretation: under no or light alignment, sibling propositions remain much closer than parent--child or unrelated propositions, whereas high alignment compresses the latent geometry and weakens structural separation.

The PCA projections of the learned structure latent support the quantitative results. Figure 6 shows the representative $\lambda$ = 0.03, seed 0 structure latent coloured by language. German and English samples occupy the same broad latent space rather than separating into isolated language clusters. Figure 7 shows the same representative structure latent coloured by hierarchy depth. The visible depth organisation indicates that the shared bilingual structure latent retains hierarchy-relevant information while aligning German and English proposition samples. These PCA figures are diagnostic visualisations rather than aggregate evidence; the main claims rest on the ten-seed metrics reported in Tables 2--6.

Taken together, the bilingual results provide controlled evidence that explicit formal structure in the Tractatus can be encoded in a compact latent space and shared across bilingual textual realisations. The result should be read narrowly: the model is not shown to understand Wittgenstein or to learn general language-invariant logic. Rather, under aligned German and English proposition texts and shared hierarchy supervision, the split-latent model learns a proposition-level structure representation that supports both hierarchy prediction and cross-language retrieval.

\section{Discussion}
The study shows that the numbered hierarchy of the Tractatus can act as weak supervision for learning an interpretable structure latent. This structure latent can also support bilingual alignment between English and German versions of the same proposition. The main contribution of this study is therefore methodological as it shows that a formally organised philosophical text can be used to train and evaluate a compact neural latent representation of textual hierarchy.

The Tractatus is usually suitable for structural modelling because its proposition numbers encode formal relations. These relations include depth, parent propositions local families, sequence, and child structure. The paper uses these structural relations as weak supervision and the GRU VAE model learns a split-latent space. This space is decomposed into two components: a text latent for reconstruction and a structure latent for hierarchy prediction. Hierarchy-relevant information is recoverable from the learned structure latent. The results show that the numbering system is not merely an editorial feature of the text, but a viable formal scaffold for representation learning.

The English-only model successfully learned useful information about proposition hierarchy. Depth prediction was strongest, suggesting that broad hierarchical level is easiest to encode. Parent prediction was also meaningful, suggesting the structure latent captured local hierarchy, not just depth. Next-proposition prediction was weaker, suggesting that fine-grained sequential order is harder to recover. Sibling-distance diagnostics support the idea that the latent geometry reflects hierarchy. On this basis, the structure latent appears to encode formal position rather than simply than simply acting as an additional reconstruction latent.

In the bilingual work, English and German versions of the same numbered proposition become aligned in the structure latent. This happens even when explicit language alignment is absent, showing that shared structural targets are enough to pull the two languages into a common formal-position space. This means the model necessarily aligns propositions through shared hierarchy, not through loss-based translation supervision. In other words, because both language versions share the same proposition number and hierarchy-derived targets, the model can learn a common structure space despite language-based differences and in the absence of the alignment loss.

Concerning alignment, light alignment gave the best or near-best retrieval, whereas performance became impaired as alignment increased, with high alignment degrading both text retrieval and hierarchy prediction. The structure latent needs to strike a balance between possessing enough invariance to align English and German version and enough discrimination to preserve fine-grained formal distinctions. I hypothesise, therefore, that too alignment may collapse neighbouring positions together, weakening the model's ability to distinguish proposition position. It follows that, the best model does not force maximum cross-language similarity, but preserves enough distinct latent structure to keep nearby propositions separate.

When directional balance was considered, English-to-German and German-to-English retrieval were similar. This suggests the model was not simply English-centred with German merely projected into an English latent space. Equivalently, it is also against a German-centred model with a German dominated latent space. Balanced retrieval in both directions supports the interpretation that the bilingual structure latent is shared rather than dominated by one language.

Critically, it can be seen that the split-latent architecture adopted gives the model a division of textual reconstruction and propositional position prediction. The text latent is fed into the decoder whereas the structure latent is pressured by hierarchy-prediction heads. The single latent VAE may reconstruct text but lacks the capacity to effectively isolate formal hierarchy. Consequently, the split-latent leads to an interpretable structure latent and makes it possible to probe how hierarchy is encoded separately from surface reconstruction. The architectural separation between text and structure does not guarantee perfect disentanglement, but it makes the hierarchy-carrying component measurable and interpretable.

This work of computational text analysis draws on representation learning and shows that ordered texts with explicit formal organisation can be studied computationally. It provides a method for probing structure in text, rather than solely focusing on semantics. As such it treats the Tractatus as a constrained test case for learning latent hierarchy. It may thus be useful for other formally organised texts across philosophical, theological, legal and mathematical domains where a computational method for representing and testing formal textual structure is indicated.

There are five key limitations which qualify this work. Firstly, the model's success depends on the presence of an explicit numbering scaffold providing supervision. That is to say, the hierarchy is derived from the numbering system rather than discovered from the text alone. As has been remarked before, the model is learning under weak supervision. This caution is important because latent factors should not be treated as unsupervised discoveries without inductive biases or supervision.\textsuperscript{9} Secondly, limitations of the Tractatus as a corpus arise from the unusual structure of the text. The Tractatus possesses a formal hierarchy which sets it aside from other documents. Transfer of the techniques developed in this paper to less structured texts is uncertain. Thirdly, a limit to interpretation is that the latent structure in the model should not be treated as capable of philosophical understanding. Instead, it captures formal position not necessarily logical meaning. Fourth, bilingual limitations arise since the bilingual task uses aligned proposition-IDs rather than open-ended translation or general multilingual reasoning. Fifth, limitations in modelling are apparent. The GRU encoder-decoder architecture is relatively basic in its construction and ability to transform data and the structure heads capture only selected features. Correspondingly, next-proposition prediction remains comparatively weak. Later in the analysis pipeline conclusions drawn on the PCA visualisations can also only be tentative since these visualisations are diagnostic but not definite proof of structure.

Future work could test whether the learned structure latent generalises beyond seen proposition families, or whether it primarily memorises the local geometry of the numbered corpus. This would be achieved most effectively through hold-out testing, for example by holding back selected proposition families and evaluating whether their structural positions can still be recovered. A complementary vein of work would focus on crafting stronger relational objectives for parent-child and sibling structure. As such, one could replace or augment the recurrent VAE with graph-based architectures that explicitly encode the proposition tree. Graph neural networks are designed to learn from node features and graph structure, making this a natural extension.\textsuperscript{10} In such a model, propositions would be represented as nodes and formal relations such as parenthood, siblinghood, sequence, and bilingual equivalence would be represented as edges. This would allow the model to learn proposition representations through message passing over the actual structure of the Tractatus, rather than only predicting structural labels from individual proposition texts. A second architectural extension would be to replace the GRU encoder with a Transformer-based encoder. Whereas the present GRU model compresses each proposition through recurrent sequence processing, a Transformer encoder could use self-attention to model longer-range token dependencies within each proposition and may provide richer text representations before projection into the text and structure latents.\textsuperscript{11} In the present corpus, however, this would need to be tested carefully under the same family-level hold-out regime, since a higher-capacity encoder could improve generalisation but could also overfit the small numbered corpus. Further examination of whether latent dimensions correspond to interpretable structural features may also be fruitful, as would testing with other hierarchical and formally organised texts.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper showed that the numbered structure of the Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus can be used as weak supervision for learning proposition hierarchy. In the monolingual model, the structure latent recovered depth, parenthood, and local hierarchy while remaining separate from the reconstruction-oriented text latent. In the bilingual model, English and German versions of the same proposition aligned strongly in the shared structure latent space. The alignment-weight sweep showed that strong alignment pressure can damage fine-grained hierarchy. The best structure latent space balances language invariance with proposition-level discrimination. The result should be interpreted as a model of formal textual structure, not philosophical understanding. The broader contribution is to show that structured philosophical texts can be used as constrained test cases for interpretable neural latent representation learning. Ultimately, the Tractatus provides a useful test case for studying how textual hierarchy, latent representation and bilingual alignment interact under a constrained computational model.
