Bilingual Structure Latents in the Tractatus: Aligning German and English
Propositions with Shared Logical Supervision

Author: Mark Alfred Griffiths


ABSTRACT

This paper describes a single combined study of learned proposition-structure latents in
Wittgenstein's Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus. The work begins with a
monolingual split-latent variational autoencoder and extends it to a bilingual
German-English setting. The central question is whether the hierarchical
numbering system of the Tractatus can be used as weak supervision for learning a
compact latent representation of formal proposition position, and whether that
representation can align German and English realizations of the same numbered
proposition.

The dataset contains 526 numbered propositions. In the monolingual study, each
row contains English text and hierarchy-derived targets. In the bilingual study,
each proposition has both English and German text, giving 1052 flattened
language-specific samples. The retained model uses a split latent space: a
24-dimensional text latent for reconstruction and an 8-dimensional structure
latent for hierarchy prediction. In bilingual runs, the decoder is conditioned on
language, while the structure targets are shared across German and English by
proposition id.

The monolingual result motivates the architecture. A single 32-dimensional VAE
latent reconstructs text but produces weak hierarchy geometry: nearest-neighbor
family agreement is 3.0 percent. Splitting the latent into text and structure
subspaces produces a compact structure representation in which local geometry
tracks the Tractatus hierarchy. The retained monolingual seed sweep uses the
balanced reg005 configuration across seeds 0..9.

The bilingual result extends this finding. Across ten seeds and five alignment
strengths, cross-language proposition retrieval is already strong with no
explicit alignment loss: top-1 retrieval is 0.9348 +/- 0.0115 and mean
reciprocal rank is 0.9650 +/- 0.0066. Light alignment at
lambda_language_alignment = 0.03 gives the strongest mean retrieval and hierarchy
scores: top-1 retrieval is 0.9365 +/- 0.0103, MRR is 0.9662 +/- 0.0058, parent
accuracy is 0.8608 +/- 0.0094, depth accuracy is 0.9643 +/- 0.0136, and
next-proposition accuracy is 0.6189 +/- 0.0219. High alignment at 1.00 degrades
retrieval and hierarchy. The result does not show that the model understands
Wittgenstein or learns general
language-invariant logic. It shows a narrower result: under aligned German and
English proposition texts, and under shared hierarchy supervision, the learned
structure latent tracks formal proposition position across languages.


1. INTRODUCTION

The Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus is not arranged like an ordinary essay. Its
propositions are numbered hierarchically. Proposition 1 is followed by
subordinate propositions such as 1.1, 1.11, and 1.12. Proposition 4.12 is
elaborated by descendants such as 4.121, 4.1211, and 4.1212. Proposition 5 is
related to propositions beginning with 5.1, 5.2, and so on. The numbering is not
merely decorative. It gives the book a formal scaffold: parent relations, depth,
siblings, descendants, and textual successor relations.

This paper asks a machine-learning question about that scaffold. Can a model
learn a latent representation that reflects proposition position in the
Tractatus hierarchy, rather than simply reconstructing text? More directly: can
the numbering system be used as weak supervision for making the formal
organization of the work visible in a learned latent space?

The question is useful because the Tractatus is small but unusually structured.
It is not large enough to train a general language model in the usual modern
sense. But it is rich in formal relations. Each numbered proposition has a
position in a tree. It may have a parent, children, siblings, ancestors, and a
next proposition in reading order. These relations can be derived from the
numbering system and treated as structural targets.

The monolingual study tests whether these targets can be represented in a
compact latent space for the English text. The bilingual study asks a sharper
follow-up question. If the model sees German and English versions of the same
propositions, and if the hierarchy targets are shared by proposition id, does the
structure latent align the two languages by proposition identity?

This creates a simple operational test. If the structure latent is only an
English lexical embedding, then German and English versions of the same
proposition should not necessarily appear near one another in structure space.
If, however, the structure latent is carrying proposition-level formal position,
then the German and English realizations of the same proposition should align.

The central claim is deliberately modest:

    The bilingual split-latent model learns proposition-level structure latents
    that align German and English realizations under shared Tractatus hierarchy
    supervision.

This should not be inflated into a claim about general language-invariant logic.
The dataset is aligned by proposition id. The supervision comes from the
Tractatus numbering system. The evaluation is in-sample on a structured corpus.
The result is evidence for formal-position tracking in this setting, not for
general philosophical understanding.


2. DATASET AND STRUCTURAL TARGETS

The monolingual dataset is stored at:

    tractatus_structure_latents/data/tractatus.json

The bilingual dataset is stored at:

    tractatus_structure_latents/data/tractatus_bilingual.json

Both datasets contain one structural row per numbered proposition. The
monolingual row contains English text. The bilingual row contains both German
and English text under a texts object. The bilingual dataset is built from the
Project Gutenberg Tractatus source by extracting German and English proposition
entries and aligning them by proposition number. A simplified bilingual row has
this form:

    parent_id: "4.12"
    depth: 4
    texts.en: "Propositions cannot represent the logical form..."
    texts.de: "Die Saetze koennen die logische Form nicht darstellen..."
    next_id: "4.1211"
    children: ["4.1211", "4.1212"]
    siblings: ["4.122", "4.123"]
    ancestor_chain: ["4", "4.1", "4.12"]
    child_count: 2

The retained corpus has the following proposition-level statistics:

    proposition rows:          526
    bilingual text samples:    1052
    languages:                 de, en
    first proposition:          1
    last proposition:           7

Depth distribution:

    depth 1:   7
    depth 2:   25
    depth 3:   124
    depth 4:   245
    depth 5:   118
    depth 6:   7

    section 1:   7
    section 2:   79
    section 3:   74
    section 4:   109
    section 5:   151
    section 6:   105
    section 7:   1

The schema matters. The model does not receive an unstructured bilingual corpus.
It receives aligned proposition rows. German and English texts are different
surface realizations, but the proposition id and hierarchy targets are shared.
The experiment is therefore a test of proposition-level structural alignment,
not unsupervised translation.

The structural targets used in the experiments are:

    parent proposition
    hierarchy depth
    next proposition in reading order
    child count

These are formal targets derived from the numbering system. They do not exhaust
the philosophical content of the Tractatus, but they provide a precise and
machine-checkable version of its visible organization.


3. MODEL

The model is a recurrent variational autoencoder. A VAE reads an input,
compresses it into a latent representation, and reconstructs the input from that
representation. In this project, the input is one proposition text at a time.

The basic flow is:

    proposition text
      -> encoder
      -> latent representation
      -> decoder
      -> reconstructed proposition text

The key design choice is to split the latent representation into two parts:

    z_text:       24 dimensions
    z_structure:  8 dimensions

The decoder receives z_text. The hierarchy heads receive z_structure. In the
retained model, the decoder does not receive z_structure. This makes the division
of labour explicit:

    z_text       -> language-conditioned decoder -> reconstructed text
    z_structure  -> parent head
    z_structure  -> depth head
    z_structure  -> next-proposition head
    z_structure  -> child-count head

The split is important because a single latent vector is a poor interpretive
object when it is asked to do everything at once. For text reconstruction, the
latent should preserve word choice, topic, and local phrasing. For hierarchy
prediction, the latent should preserve formal role: whether the proposition is a
top-level claim, a subordinate elaboration, a member of a local family, or a
transition to the next proposition. These roles overlap only partly.

The bilingual model keeps the split but adds language conditioning. The decoder
receives a learned language embedding, so it knows whether it is reconstructing
German or English. Structural targets remain shared across languages by
proposition id.

The bilingual training objective is:

    L = reconstruction_loss
      + beta_text * KL(z_text)
      + beta_structure * KL(z_structure)
      + lambda_parent * parent_loss
      + lambda_depth * depth_loss
      + lambda_next * next_loss
      + lambda_child * child_count_loss
      + lambda_language_alignment * alignment_loss

The optional alignment term is applied to same-proposition German and English
structure latents:

    L_align = mse(z_structure_en(id), z_structure_de(id))

The retained bilingual sweep holds the architecture and ordinary structure
weights fixed while varying lambda_language_alignment:

    z_text:          24
    z_structure:      8
    epochs:          80
    batch_size:      32
    beta_text:     0.01
    beta_structure: 0.05
    lambda_parent: 0.2
    lambda_depth:  0.1
    lambda_next:   0.2
    lambda_child:  0.02

The tested alignment strengths are:

    lambda_language_alignment = 0.00, 0.03, 0.10, 0.30, 1.00


4. MONOLINGUAL SPLIT-LATENT STUDY

The monolingual study establishes why the split-latent architecture is needed.
The first model used a single 32-dimensional latent vector. It could reconstruct
text, but its latent space did not produce useful hierarchy geometry. Its
nearest-neighbor family agreement was only 3.0 percent. In practical terms, if
one takes a proposition and asks which other proposition is closest to it in the
baseline latent space, that neighbor is almost never from the same local
proposition family.

The split-latent model keeps the total latent size at 32 dimensions but assigns
24 dimensions to text reconstruction and 8 dimensions to hierarchy prediction.
This makes the result interpretable. If the 8-dimensional structure latent
clusters by depth, top-level section, or local family, then hierarchy is present
in the structure subspace rather than merely entangled with the decoder's
word-prediction features.

The retained monolingual configuration is the balanced reg005 model:

    z_text:        24
    z_structure:   8
    beta_text:   0.01
    beta_structure: 0.05
    seeds:       0..9

The canonical artifact root is:

    runs/seed_sweeps/monolingual_split_24_8_reg005/

The current monolingual seed-sweep summary is:

    seeds:                 10
    parent accuracy:       0.6983 +/- 0.0152
    depth accuracy:        0.9274 +/- 0.0275
    next accuracy:         0.3724 +/- 0.0198
    reconstruction loss:   1.2923 +/- 0.0194
    perplexity:            3.6416 +/- 0.0706
    KL structure:          8.9653 +/- 0.1127
    sibling distance:      2.0656 +/- 0.0342
    parent-child distance: 6.1065 +/- 0.1566
    unrelated distance:    5.5574 +/- 0.0532

The distance metrics are useful because they check latent geometry directly.
Siblings are much closer than unrelated propositions in the retained
monolingual structure space. This supports the same architectural lesson as the
older KNN analysis: the split structure latent carries formal hierarchy
information rather than acting only as a reconstruction feature.

5. BILINGUAL ALIGNMENT STUDY

The bilingual study extends the monolingual result by adding German proposition
texts. Each numbered proposition now has two text realizations. The German and
English strings differ lexically and syntactically, but they share proposition
id and hierarchy targets. During training, the model reconstructs each text in
its own language while using the same hierarchy supervision for both languages.

The central evaluation asks whether the structure latent aligns same-id German
and English propositions. For each German structure latent, the evaluator
retrieves the nearest English structure latent and checks whether it has the
same proposition id. The same calculation is also done in the English-to-German
direction.

The main bilingual retrieval metrics are:

    cross_language_top1_id_accuracy
    cross_language_mrr
    mean_same_id_cross_language_distance

The hierarchy metrics are:

    parent accuracy
    depth accuracy
    next-proposition accuracy

The evaluation also reports reconstruction loss and perplexity, but these are
secondary for this paper. Reconstruction can be good while the structure latent
is poor. The important question is whether z_structure supports both hierarchy
prediction and cross-language proposition retrieval.

Several points matter.

First, explicit alignment is not required for strong cross-language retrieval.
At lambda_language_alignment = 0.00, top-1 proposition retrieval is 0.9348 +/-
0.0115 and MRR is 0.9650 +/- 0.0066. Shared hierarchy supervision alone is
therefore sufficient to induce substantial bilingual alignment in z_structure.

Second, light alignment is the best-supported setting in the ten-seed sweep. At
lambda_language_alignment = 0.03, top-1 retrieval is 0.9365 +/- 0.0103 and MRR
is 0.9662 +/- 0.0058. Parent, depth, and next-proposition accuracies are also
among the strongest values in the sweep.

Third, the previous single-run emphasis on lambda_language_alignment = 0.10
should be treated as superseded by the seed sweep. On average, 0.03 is better
supported than 0.10, so the representative PCA plots use align003 seed 0.

Fourth, high alignment damages the model. At lambda_language_alignment = 1.00,
mean top-1 retrieval falls to 0.7943 +/- 0.0188, MRR falls to 0.8577 +/- 0.0130,
parent accuracy falls to 0.5736, depth accuracy falls to 0.7984, and
next-proposition accuracy falls to 0.3614.

The result is therefore not that more alignment is always better. The better
interpretation is that z_structure needs enough shared pressure to place same-id
German and English propositions in compatible regions, but not so much pressure
that it collapses distinctions needed for hierarchy prediction.

8. PROPOSITION TRACKING AND FORMAL LOGICAL POSITION

The most important interpretive question is what bilingual alignment means. It
is tempting to say that the model has learned language-invariant logic. That is
too strong.

The safer interpretation is proposition tracking under shared formal
supervision. The Tractatus numbering system encodes a formal organization of the
book. Proposition ids are not arbitrary labels in the ordinary sense; they mark
positions in a hierarchy. If German and English versions of proposition 4.121
are close in z_structure, and if z_structure also supports parent, depth, and
next prediction, then the latent is tracking something about the proposition's
formal role in the Tractatus.

This is not the same as philosophical understanding. The model is not shown to
understand what proposition 4.121 means. It is not shown to reason from that
proposition to another. It is not shown to generalize to unseen philosophical
texts. It is shown to place two language realizations of the same numbered
proposition near one another in a learned structure space trained on the shared
hierarchy.

That result still matters. It answers a concrete objection to the monolingual
paper. If the English-only structure latent were merely a lexical embedding,
then introducing German should disrupt proposition-level alignment. Instead,
the bilingual results show strong alignment, including in the no-explicit-
alignment setting.

The best description is therefore:

    z_structure tracks proposition-level formal position across German and
    English realizations under shared Tractatus hierarchy supervision.

This is a meaningful result about the geometry of the learned representation. It
is not a claim that the model appreciates logic in the human philosophical
sense.


9. DISCUSSION

The combined study supports three main conclusions.

First, the split-latent architecture is necessary for interpretability. The
single-latent VAE reconstructs text but has weak hierarchy geometry. Splitting
text reconstruction and hierarchy prediction into separate latent subspaces
makes it possible to inspect whether formal structure is present in a dedicated
8-dimensional z_structure.

Second, the bilingual result is not merely an English-side artifact. The model
reconstructs German and English through language-conditioned decoding, while the
structural heads operate on a shared z_structure. Cross-language retrieval is
high even without an explicit bilingual alignment loss.

Third, explicit alignment is useful only within limits. Light alignment performs
well. High alignment damages both retrieval and hierarchy. This is consistent
with the purpose of z_structure: it must align same-id language realizations,
but it must also keep different propositions apart in ways that preserve formal
relations.

The result also clarifies the role of reconstruction. Reconstruction loss and
perplexity are useful diagnostics, but they are not sufficient for judging the
structure model. A setting can reconstruct text well while weakening retrieval
or hierarchy prediction. The latent is valuable here because of its geometry and
its structural predictive use, not merely because it helps decode words.


10. LIMITATIONS

The limitations are substantial.

The dataset is small. It contains 526 propositions and 1052 flattened bilingual
samples. This is enough for a controlled proof-of-principle experiment, but not
enough to support broad claims about multilingual reasoning.

The German and English texts are aligned by proposition id. This is a favorable
setting for cross-language retrieval. The experiment does not show that the
model could discover such alignments from unpaired text.

The structural supervision is derived from the Tractatus numbering system. The
model is not discovering the hierarchy from raw text alone. It is learning a
latent representation under explicit weak supervision from parent, depth, next,
and child-count targets.

The evaluation is in-sample. The reported retrieval and hierarchy metrics do not
establish out-of-sample generalization to unseen propositions, unseen sections,
or other philosophical works.

The result is specific to the Tractatus. The book is unusually structured, and
its German and English versions are parallel realizations of the same numbered
work. Other texts may not provide comparable formal scaffolding.

The model uses a simple word-level setup rather than a modern multilingual
tokenizer or pretrained multilingual encoder. This is useful for interpretability,
but it limits direct comparison with contemporary multilingual language models.

Finally, the philosophical interpretation must remain modest. The model does not
understand Wittgenstein. It does not prove language-invariant logic. It learns a
compact structure latent that aligns bilingual proposition realizations under
shared hierarchy supervision.


11. CONCLUSION

This paper combines the monolingual and bilingual Tractatus latent-structure
studies into a single account. The monolingual study shows why the split-latent
architecture is needed: a single latent can reconstruct text without producing a
useful hierarchy, while a dedicated 8-dimensional structure latent can represent
formal proposition position. The bilingual study then shows that this structure
latent can align German and English realizations of the same propositions.

The strongest bilingual evidence comes from the ten-seed alignment sweep. With
no explicit alignment loss, cross-language top-1 retrieval is 0.9348 +/- 0.0115
and MRR is 0.9650 +/- 0.0066. With light alignment at
lambda_language_alignment = 0.03, top-1 retrieval is 0.9365 +/- 0.0103 and MRR
is 0.9662 +/- 0.0058. High alignment at 1.00 degrades both hierarchy prediction
and retrieval.

The conclusion is therefore precise. The model has not learned general logic and
has not understood Wittgenstein. In this aligned bilingual Tractatus setting,
the split-latent model learns a proposition-level structural representation
shared across German and English realizations.


12. FUTURE WORK

The immediate next step is held-out evaluation. Section-level or proposition-
level splits would test whether the learned structure representation generalizes
beyond the exact propositions used in training.

A second step is to compare against multilingual sentence embeddings. Such a
comparison would help distinguish what is gained by the explicit split-latent
architecture and hierarchy supervision.

A third step is to test weaker alignment conditions, including partial bilingual
coverage, missing translations, or unpaired propositions.

A fourth step is to extend the dataset to additional translations if reliable
public-domain sources are available. This would test whether the structural
latent remains stable beyond German and English.

A fifth step is to train a latent dynamics model for predicting z_structure at
the next proposition. This would test whether the learned structure space can
support sequential modelling of the book's formal progression.

Finally, similar methods could be applied to other formally organized texts. The
key requirement is not that the text be philosophical, but that it have a
meaningful explicit structure that can be used as weak supervision.
